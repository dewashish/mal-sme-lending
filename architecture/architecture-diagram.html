§ 03 · Decision Engine

The Decision Engine

This is the heart of the platform. Underwriting, pricing, EWS, fraud, collections routing, pre-approval all run through the same five steps. Each step composes some mix of rules, policies, manual workflow, referrals, and models. The model class depends on the question.

step 01

Data pull

AECB, e-invoice match, bank feed, KYC docs, POS, prior platform footprint. Parallel where possible, cached behind the feature store.
data
step 02

Feature build

Canonical features: cashflow ratios, exposure, sector, seasonality, behaviour. Versioned, lineage-tracked.
features
step 03

Score

Whatever model class fits the question. Behavioural and deep models for acquisition. Scoring families for underwriting. Survival for EWS. Output is a signal, not a decision.
models
step 04

Policy gate

Deterministic rules. Sharia, sanctions, exposure caps, DSCR floors, KYC pass / fail. Binary, auditable. Overrides the model in either direction.
rules
step 05

Price + decide

Risk-tier price, limit, plan ladder, customer-facing reason codes (SHAP for the regulator, plain language for the customer).
decide
applies to

Same engine, many questions

Acquisition · onboarding · underwriting · pricing · in-life limit review · EWS · fraud · collections routing. One engine, one trace log, one audit pack. Built in-house from day one. Risk team configures rules without engineering lift.
build · day 1
R

Rules

Sanctions, blacklist, minimum eligibility, exposure caps, DSCR floors, sector concentration. Binary. Regulator-defensible.
P

Policies

Risk-team-owned, configurable in the engine without engineering lift. Versioned. Approved by the Credit Forum.
M

Models

PD, LGD, EWS, fraud, doc verification, propensity, look-alike. Model class chosen by task. SHAP for every decision.
Mn

Manual

Any case the AI scores below threshold, any ticket above Express ceiling, any first-time pair above policy, any sector flagged by the SSB.
Rf

Referrals

Out-of-policy cases route to named committees with SLAs. Sharia structure to SSB. Cross-product to Credit Forum. Nothing falls through.
⚙

Always on

Every decision logs input snapshot, model version, policy hash, manual overrides. Queryable by the regulator on request.
§ 04 · Build vs Buy

Build over buy

The cost of building software has collapsed. The cost of being locked into someone else's product roadmap has not. We build the parts of the stack that touch our customers, our policy, or our IP. We buy where the regulator mandates the counterparty, where the value is genuinely ecosystem-wide (fraud, sanctions), or where the system is a true commodity.

B+

Build, Day 1

Decision Engine · LOS · Pricing Engine · Murabaha contract engine · Customer graph · Payments orchestrator · Customer apps · Accounting connectors (5 core) · Onboarding orchestration · Conversational AI (built on managed LLMs) · Portfolio monitoring · EWS · Expense policy engine
core IP
B*

License, then migrate

LMS (Mambu at launch, then in-house) · Collections platform (manual at launch, then in-house once NPL volume justifies) · FX engine (vendor liquidity, hedging policy ours)
de-risk launch
Bu

Buy

AECB (mandated) · KYC (Sumsub) · Sanctions / PEP (Refinitiv + Dow Jones) · Fraud intelligence (vendor, hub and spoke) · Commodity broker (DMCC / Bursa) · Card rails (Nymcard) · Open banking (Lean) · Peppol ASP (Avalara + RTC Suite) · Cloud (AWS UAE) · LLM serving (Bedrock) · Observability · EDR
commodity or mandated
⚖

Why buy fraud, even though we are build-first

Fraud is hub-and-spoke. A vendor integrated across hundreds of institutions sees a compromised IP, a synthetic identity, or a spoofed device days before any individual lender catches it. That network effect cannot be replicated by building. The vendor sits next to our own fraud signals, not in place of them.
✱

The composability commitment

Every vendor module sits behind a clean API contract from day one. The migration to an in-house module is a backend switch, not a re-architecture. Vendor and in-house can run side by side in shadow mode before cut-over. This is what makes the licence-then-migrate pattern safe.

Note on Sharia structure. Mal does not lend cash at interest. It buys the receivable from the supplier and sells the underlying obligation to the buyer at a disclosed cost-plus markup with deferred settlement (Murabaha). The Sharia Supervisory Board's standing fatwa governs the contract template; each transaction's structure is logged and re-affirmed quarterly.

§ 06 · Sharia lifecycle

Murabaha decision flow

The eight-stage gate that every invoice-finance request walks through. Yellow gates are decisions; blue gates are actions; green gates create or extinguish an asset.

01 · decision

Halal screening

Industry-code check on supplier & buyer. Block alcohol, gambling, conventional finance, adult, weapons.
SCH · screen
02 · decision

Asset existence

Invoice references a real, deliverable good or service. Reject services that are pure money-for-money.
SCH · asset
03 · action

Offer to buyer

Cost-plus quote. Discloses Mal's purchase cost, profit margin (markup), tenor, EMI schedule.
SCH · disclosure
04 · action

Promise to purchase

Buyer's binding promise (wa'd). UAE Pass signed. Mal commits to buy from supplier first.
SCH · wa'd
05 · asset

Mal acquires receivable

Mal pays the supplier (Aani). Ownership of the receivable transfers to Mal. JE-1 posted.
SCH · own
06 · asset

Murabaha sale

Mal sells the underlying obligation to the buyer at cost + markup. JE-2 posted.
SCH · sell
07 · action

EMI collection

Deferred-payment instalments. Late fees go to charity (not Mal income). Restructure available.
SCH · service
08 · asset

Contract closure

Receivable extinguished. Profit recognised. Records archived for 10 years; quarterly Sharia audit picks 5% sample.
SCH · close
⚖

Sharia Supervisory Board

Three external scholars (AAOIFI-certified) + an internal head. Quarterly fatwa review, annual product certification, sample audit on 5% of executed Murabaha contracts.
AAOIFIQuarterly
μ

§ 07 · Deployment
